EXAM FOR THE COURSE:

The only medical entrance exam for medical students after 12th in India is the National Eligibility cum Entrance Test (NEET). NEET is a single, national-level entrance examination conducted by the National Testing Agency (NTA) for admission to undergraduate medical programs in India. NEET is a mandatory entrance exam for admission to MBBS, BDS, BAMS, BUMS, BHMS, and other undergraduate medical courses in all government and private medical colleges in India. NEET is a pen-and-paper based test that is conducted for three hours. The exam consists of 200 multiple-choice questions from Physics, Chemistry, and Biology. The questions are based on the NCERT syllabus for Class 11th and 12th.
To be eligible for NEET, candidates must have passed Class 12th with PCB (Physics, Chemistry, and Biology) as major subjects. Candidates must also have scored at least 50% in PCB in their Class 12th board exams. Reserved category candidates (SC, ST, and OBC) are required to score at least 40% in PCB in their Class 12th board exams. NEET is a highly competitive exam, and the cut-off marks for admission to medical colleges are very high. To get into a good medical college, candidates need to score at least 600 marks out of 720 in NEET. Here are some tips for preparing for NEET:
• Start preparing early. NEET is a vast syllabus, so it is important to start preparing early.
• Create a study plan and stick to it.
• Use NCERT textbooks as your primary study material.
• Practice solving previous year's NEET question papers.
• Take mock tests to assess your preparation level.

SUBJECTS FOR THE MEDICAL STUDENTS:

Medical students are required to study a wide range of subjects, including:
• Basic sciences: These subjects provide the foundation for understanding the human body and how it works. Basic science subjects include anatomy, physiology, biochemistry, and cell biology. • Clinical sciences: These subjects teach students how to diagnose and treat diseases. Clinical science subjects include pathology, pharmacology, microbiology, and immunology. • Social and behavioral sciences: These subjects teach students about the social and psychological factors that can affect health. Social and behavioral science subjects include medical ethics, public health, and behavioral psychology.
In addition to these core subjects, medical students may also choose to study elective subjects in areas such as sports medicine, dermatology, or oncology.
Here is a more detailed list of the subjects that medical students typically study:
• Year 1: Anatomy, physiology, biochemistry, cell biology, behavioral psychology, medical ethics, and public health.
• Year 2: Histology, embryology, pharmacology, microbiology, immunology, and pathology.
• Year 3: Internal medicine, surgery, pediatrics, obstetrics and gynecology, psychiatry, and family medicine.
• Year 4: Clinical rotations in various medical specialties, such as neurology, radiology, and dermatology.
• Year 5: Internship in one or more medical specialties.
After completing their internship, medical students are awarded their Doctor of Medicine (MD) degree.

INTERNSHIP-APPORTUNITY:

Here are some internship opportunities for medical students:
• Hospital internships: Hospitals are the most common place for medical students to complete internships. Hospital internships can provide students with experience in a variety of medical specialties, such as internal medicine, surgery, pediatrics, and obstetrics and gynecology.
• Research internships: Research internships provide students with the opportunity to work on cutting-edge medical research projects. Research internships can be conducted at universities, hospitals, or research institutes.
• Public health internships: Public health internships allow students to work on projects that improve the health of a community or population. Public health internships can be conducted at government agencies, non-profit organizations, or research institutes.
• International internships: International internships provide students with the opportunity to learn about different healthcare systems and cultures. International internships can be conducted at hospitals, clinics, or research institutes in other countries.
To find internship opportunities, medical students can contact their school's career services office, search online job boards, and reach out to hospitals, clinics, and research institutes directly.
Here are some tips for applying for medical internships:
• Tailor your resume and cover letter to each internship you apply for. Be sure to highlight your skills and experience that are relevant to the specific internship.
• Be prepared to talk about your career goals and why you are interested in the internship. In your job interviews, be sure to explain what you hope to learn from the internship and how it will help you achieve your career goals.
• Get involved in extracurricular activities and research projects. This will help you develop your skills and experience, and it will also make you more competitive for internships.
• Network with other medical students, doctors, and healthcare professionals. This is a great way to learn about internship opportunities and get your foot in the door.
